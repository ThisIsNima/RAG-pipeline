Alcohol and Public Health. By James Eidge, M.D., and 
M.D. (State Medicine), B.S., &c., Lond., Medical Officer of 
Health, Enfield. 
London: H. K. Lewis. 1892. 
We appreciate any argument which is likely to control the 
craving of civilised man for intoxicants. We have even some 
sympathy with the aims of those who propose to put some 
limitation on the freedom with which this craving may be 
satisfied. 
But when, in regard to alcohol, we are asked to 
reconstruct our moral law, and place it on a physiological basis, 
we dissent. 
" I have long had the conviction that unless the 
practice of total abstinence is physiologically right, it cannot be 
morally binding upon all." 
This is a quotation from Dr. 
Ridge's preface, and sufficiently indicates the contents of the 
following pages. On this opinion we take direct issue with 
him. 
The question is primarily and essentially one of 
morality, and drunkenness would be quite as much a moral 
obliquity and crime were the pathological results as evanescent 
as the physiological.
